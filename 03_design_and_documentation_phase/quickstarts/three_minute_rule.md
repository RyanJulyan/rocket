# The 3-Minute Rule: Say Less to Get More from Any Pitch or Presentation
> Adapted from The Three-Minute Rule by Brant Pinvidic

This quickstart helps you structure pitches and presentations so your audience understands your idea fast. The first impression is formed in under three minutes, so your job is not to impress with volume, but to communicate with precision.

## Goal
Get clear understanding and forward momentum in the first three minutes of any pitch, proposal, or presentation.

## Core Principle
You are not trying to sound smart. You are trying to transfer understanding.

- Less information, better structured, wins
- Clarity creates trust
- Confused people do not buy
- Information first, engagement second

## Common Misconceptions

### 1. More flair means more persuasion
It is usually the opposite. Too much style, jargon, and slide noise reduces comprehension.

### 2. More detail means more credibility
Too much detail too early creates cognitive overload. The audience needs a clear map before they need depth.

### 3. If people are silent, they understand
Silence often means confusion. Build in simple checks for understanding.

## The 3-Minute Structure

### 1. Start with a clear outcome
State what this is, who it is for, and what outcome it creates.

Template:
- We help [audience] get [specific outcome] by [simple mechanism].

### 2. Use bullets, not dumps
Distill your idea into concise points. If a point is not essential to first-pass understanding, remove it.

### 3. Use value statements
Convert features into value statements the audience can immediately contextualize.

Examples:
- Feature: Weekly reporting dashboard
- Value statement: You get weekly visibility into pipeline risk before revenue drops

### 4. Inform, then engage
Do not mix explanation and persuasion in the same breath.

- Inform: What it is and how it works
- Engage: Why it matters now and what to do next

### 5. Add a hook
The hook is the part that makes someone think, "That is interesting."

Hook types:
- Cost hook
- Speed hook
- Risk-reduction hook
- Emotional hook
- Competitive hook

### 6. Add an edge
The edge is the unexpected insight, mechanism, or framing your audience did not see coming.

A good edge:
- Is specific
- Is relevant to the decision
- Separates you from alternatives

### 7. Close with one concrete next step
If the audience understands but has no path forward, momentum dies.

Close options:
- Book a working session
- Start a pilot
- Review a proposal
- Approve phase 1 scope

## WACK Your Story (Narrative Compression)
Use a short narrative that creates context without drifting.

- W: What happened
- A: A problem or tension appeared
- C: Change introduced
- K: Key result achieved

Keep this section tight. The purpose is clarity and proof, not performance.

## Information vs Engagement Checklist
Before presenting, test each line:

- Is this line informing or engaging?
- If informing, is it necessary for first-pass clarity?
- If engaging, is it anchored to a clear point already explained?
- Can I say this in fewer words?

## Build Your 3-Minute Pitch

### Step 1: One-line premise
Write one sentence that describes the idea simply.

### Step 2: Three to five value statements
List the few value statements that matter most to this audience.

### Step 3: Insert hook and edge
Add one hook and one edge that are relevant to this audience.

### Step 4: Add proof
Use one short proof point (result, case, metric, or before/after).

### Step 5: End with a single CTA
Ask for exactly one next action.

## Practical Pitch Template
Use this as a first draft:

1. Context
- "You are currently dealing with [problem context]."

2. Premise
- "We help [audience] achieve [outcome] by [mechanism]."

3. Value statements
- "This matters because [value 1]."
- "It also gives you [value 2]."
- "And it reduces [risk/cost/time]."

4. Hook
- "The most important part is [hook]."

5. Edge
- "What is different here is [edge]."

6. Proof
- "In [example], this produced [result]."

7. CTA
- "If this aligns, the next step is [single clear action]."

## PowerPointless Rules
Slides should support comprehension, not replace speaking.

- One idea per slide
- Minimal text
- No reading slides aloud
- Do not use slides as handouts
- Use simple visuals that reinforce the point

If a slide cannot be explained in one sentence, simplify it.

## Delivery Rules
- Lead with clarity, not charisma
- Use plain language
- Remove jargon unless the audience needs it
- Pause between sections
- Ask one clarifying question before closing

## 10-Minute Prep Routine
Run this before any important pitch:

1. Write your one-line premise
2. Cut your content by 30%
3. Keep only 3 to 5 value statements
4. Add one hook and one edge
5. Rehearse out loud with a 3-minute timer
6. Remove any sentence that causes friction
7. Finalize one CTA

## Fast Self-Review Scorecard
Score each item 1 to 5:

- Clarity of premise
- Relevance to audience
- Strength of value statements
- Quality of hook
- Differentiation of edge
- Simplicity of CTA
- Slide discipline

Total score:
- 30 to 35: Ready to deliver
- 24 to 29: Good, refine weak sections
- Below 24: Rework structure before presenting

## What Next

- Test it in a real conversation
- Record objections or confusion points
- Iterate the 3-minute version until it feels effortless
